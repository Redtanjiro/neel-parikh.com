Case study — Product design

Futee.

A football matchmaking platform for Bangkok, built so a Sunday morning game doesn't live or die in a group chat full of "maybes."

What shipped, honestly

Futee is a Figma prototype, not a live product — so here's what's real. No invented conversion rate, no activation percentage that was never measured. Just the research and the work that produced it.

The problem

A 47-year-old who wants to find a game fast, and an 18-year-old who wants to build one — same app, opposite jobs.

Eighteen interviews with grassroots players in Bangkok collapsed into two personas who wanted almost nothing in common. Chai, 47, married, working full time, wants to open the app, find a game, and play — minimum friction, minimum decisions. Chatri, 18, a student with time to spare, wants to create games, invite people, and build a football network. One wants speed. The other wants to build something. A product that treats "create" and "find" as equally important, by default, under-serves both.

  1. 01

    Turnout — every game started as a group-chat chase

    Getting to eleven confirmed players meant pinging the same five people who hadn't replied, over and over, until kickoff.

  2. 02

    Skill — nobody knew who could actually play

    A "yes" in a group chat tells you nothing about whether someone can hold a position or is turning up in jeans. Game quality was a coin flip.

  3. 03

    Reliability — "yes" wasn't a commitment

    People said yes and didn't show. There was no visible cost to flaking, and no way to tell in advance who was likely to.

How I framed it

Two personas who wanted the same app to do opposite jobs.

This wasn't my city or my group chats. Every assumption I'd bring from Sydney was a guess, so the process moved deliberately from listening to building — 18 interviews with grassroots players in Bangkok before a single wireframe, each phase feeding the next.

  1. 01

    Listening to the brief behind the brief

    The stated ask was booking and creating games. The real ask, once I dug in, was matchmaking — Futee as the first sport on a platform meant to grow into many.

  2. 02

    Absorbing what already existed

    Stakeholder analysis, competitor booking and matchmaking apps, and the patterns already shaping how people find games online.

  3. 03

    Localising instead of assuming

    Conversations aimed squarely at the Thai market and audience — not a place to import Sydney assumptions about how people organise a kickabout.

  4. 04

    Framing two very different users

    The person creating a game and the person joining one needed different things, so I mapped each as its own persona and journey instead of one blended user.

  5. 05

    Making it real

    20 wireframe iterations, the UI system, and the prototyped flows that make up Futee today.

Their journey maps diverge in the same place they diverge in real life. Chai's mood dips at "Choose" — hunting for a venue that suits everyone — and recovers once the game's actually up. Chatri's dips at "Apply," waiting to hear whether an organiser accepts him, and peaks once he's on the pitch. Two different anxieties, at two different steps, inside what looks like one shared flow.

The constraint that never shows up in a portfolio screenshot: Futee ships bilingual, Thai and English, with a toggle live on the dashboard, search, and matchmaking screens. Writing copy and laying out information density that both languages could carry — for two personas with very different patience for reading — shaped more decisions than any single feature did.

What I killed

Six equal blocks became a nav bar, a search bar, and a card grid.

The block layout was appealing for an obvious reason: big tap targets, bold colour, nothing to misread. But it made a decision it hadn't earned — that "Create a game" and "Find a game" deserved identical visual weight. Once the personas existed as two real people with opposite goals instead of one blended "user," that decision stopped holding up. Chai doesn't want six blocks to scan through before he can find a game. Chatri doesn't want "Create" buried at the same size as everything else he'll never touch.

Navy and orange got replaced with black and yellow, and the six-block stack became a conventional top nav, a real search bar, and a card grid sorted by rating — the pattern that carries through the rest of the product.

The trade-off: the block layout would have made for a bolder portfolio screenshot. It also would have made Chai hunt through five other people's priorities to do the one thing he opened the app for. For a product that lives or dies on whether people bother opening it before a Sunday game, that trade wasn't close.

The solution

The system that replaced the group chat

One place to create and customise a game, book from 150+ grounds, and invite players with visible ratings — so a match is set up to be worth showing up for.

Turnout, skill and reliability all get solved structurally, not with a badge: Create Game and Find Game are separate paths, and pitches — like players, once you're choosing who to invite — are rated and sorted by rating by default.

Post-game: dividing players into teams

This is the piece that does the most work. A game gets 12 confirmations, but only 10 people actually show up — so the organiser first confirms who's here, then the app splits whoever's left into even teams and lets them re-shuffle by hand before kickoff. It's the one screen that has to react to reality instead of a plan.

This is also where the bilingual constraint is most visible: the TH/ENG toggle sits on the dashboard and the matchmaking screen — both dense enough that language actually changes how much fits on them.

What I'd do differently

The same screen disagrees with itself, across breakpoints, and nobody caught it until now.

Onboarding asks what position you play twice — once on desktop, once on mobile. The copy doesn't match:

Neither version is wrong on its own. Together, they're a bug — the same form field asking a different question depending on what device you're holding. I found it going back through the file for this write-up, not during the project, which is the real problem: we reviewed each platform's designs separately against the persona and the flow, but never diffed the same screen's copy across breakpoints against each other. It's a small inconsistency, but it's exactly the kind that erodes trust in a form — a user filling this out on both devices would reasonably wonder which one is correct.

What I'd change: a copy pass that's explicitly cross-device, not per-device — reading every string next to its counterpart on the other breakpoint before calling a flow done, not just checking it reads well in isolation.
