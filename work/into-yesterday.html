Case study — Speculative installation

Into Yesterday.

Visitors walk into a maze that gets warmer, taller, and louder the deeper they go — grass rising underfoot, light shifting from cool white to amber, sound building — until the walls open onto water and a scatter of glowing orbs. The descent is the design: a physical journey built to feel like remembering.

The brief

A course brief for Vivid Sydney, worked solo, resolved as a concept — not a build.

Into Yesterday was designed for DDES9010 (Immersive Design) at UNSW: translate abstract emotional states — nostalgia, warmth, reflection — into a physical journey a visitor walks through, sited at Barangaroo Reserve for Vivid Sydney. Solo scope, start to finish: concept design, spatial design, 3D visualisation, and sound design, in Blender and GarageBand.

It's academic and speculative, and that's stated here plainly rather than hedged around: the project is resolved as a spatial system, a full 3D render, and an original soundscape — not fabricated, not tested with a live audience. The status sits next to the craft, not in place of it.

Barangaroo Reserve was chosen for its relationship with water, open public access, and the contrast between urban density and calm landscape — a site already associated with pause and reflection, which the installation leans on rather than fights.

The pivot

A striking pavilion solved the wrong problem. A walked maze solved the real one.

The first two directions were architectural. Iteration 1 was a sloped-roof structure over a platform; Iteration 2 rounded that into a dome. Both were striking objects to look at. Neither solved the actual brief — a graduated emotional journey, where intensity changes as a visitor moves through it. A single enclosed structure can't zone itself: you're either inside the feeling or outside it, with no gradient in between.

The maze dropped the building idea entirely. A walked path let grass height, light warmth, air temperature, and sound intensity each shift continuously with distance travelled — short grass and bright, cool light at the edges; long grass, warm light, warm air, and a fuller soundscape toward the centre. The spatial device carries the emotional pacing; it isn't a backdrop for it.

Why it survived the cut: neither pavilion iteration could zone a journey — only frame a view. The maze could, because a path has length, and length is the one thing a single object doesn't have.

The second constraint

One entrance, built to bottleneck itself.

Vivid Sydney draws large, mixed crowds — the installation had to absorb high foot traffic without breaking the personal, reflective quality it's built around. The single-entrance maze solves this directly: it creates a natural bottleneck that self-regulates flow, so the piece can serve a large audience while each visitor still experiences it as a private, unhurried walk. The same zoning that carries the emotional arc — low immersion at the edge, rising toward a high-immersion core, releasing into the reflection area at the water — also carries the crowd logic.

The eight-frame storyboard below is what the zoning was actually planned against — arrival to reflection, each frame a checkpoint for what the light, grass, and sound should be doing at that point in the walk.

Sight, sound, touch, and movement were designed as one system, not layered on separately:

  • Sight — sunset hues, soft gradients, reflections on water.
  • Sound — a nostalgic soundscape composed in GarageBand, intensity rising toward the centre and softening again near the water.
  • Touch — grass underfoot, cool evening air against warm light.
  • Movement — gentle pulsing lights and drifting orbs guiding attention.

None of that reads as one sense at a time when you're standing in it — which is the point, and also exactly the part a render and a stereo mix can't fully prove.

The honest outcome: Into Yesterday is a course submission, not a built installation. The concept is resolved end-to-end — spatial design, a full 3D render, an original soundscape — but it hasn't gone in front of a live audience, and there's no attendance number or usability finding to report because neither happened. The craft is real; the outcome is a resolved concept, not a measured one, and that's stated here plainly rather than dressed up as something it isn't.
