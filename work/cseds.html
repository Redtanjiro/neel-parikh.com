Case study — Web design & build

CSEDS.

Two live websites for an Australian construction-materials distributor, consolidated into one — findable, faithful to what existing customers already knew, and light enough that a catalogue browsed mostly on mobile still loads.

Role
Freelance web designer — sole designer on the project
Client
CSEDS Australia Pty Ltd
Scope
Site consolidation — IA, platform selection, template customisation, content strategy
Platform
Framer, on the Manufact template
Live at
csedsaust.com.au
Outcome, first

Two sites, thirty years of history, down to one

CSEDS is live, not a prototype — csedsaust.com.au. It's too new for a performance benchmark worth quoting yet, so rather than guess at a number, here's what's real and countable instead.

The problem

Two websites, one mobile-first audience, and no room to be heavy.

CSEDS had two large, live websites that needed to become one — a construction-materials distributor's brand site and its growing engineering-software arm, both built up over years, both still getting real traffic. The brief carried four constraints, and they pull against each other. More content fights findability. Faithful-to-the-originals fights improvement. Comprehensive fights lightweight-on-mobile. There's no single move that resolves all four at once — the whole project is finding the trade-off that gets closest.

  1. 01

    Consolidate — everything from both sites, in one place

    Two live catalogues, two navigation structures, two histories of content decisions. All of it needed to land on one site without quietly dropping something a returning customer expected to find.

  2. 02

    Findable — all that information easy to navigate, not buried

    Close to nine products and three software licences don't fit on one page without becoming a wall nobody scans past the fold.

  3. 03

    Faithful — don't stray too far from the originals

    Existing users needed to still recognise it. This wasn't a rebrand brief; it was a merge, and a merge that looks unfamiliar reads as broken, not improved.

  4. 04

    Light — most users browse products on mobile

    A distributor's customers are checking a spec sheet from a site office or a truck, not a desk. A heavy page that stalls on a slow connection loses the sale before it loads.

There's no separate "what I killed" section here — there wasn't a major abandoned direction on this project. Resolving this four-way tension is the case study, the same role two opposite personas play in the Futee write-up.

How I approached it

Choosing the tool that let the client keep editing after I left.

CSEDS Australia Pty Ltd isn't an engineering consultancy — it's a distributor, bringing international construction products and engineering software into the Australian market under founder Ted Bennett, CP Eng. That distinction shaped the brief: this is a catalogue that changes as CSEDS signs new suppliers, run by a team that needed to update it themselves, not a static portfolio site.

  1. 01

    Auditing both live sites

    Cataloguing what existed on each — the product ranges, the software arm, and the content that had accumulated over years, including sample blog posts that were never real editorial, just placeholder left over from an earlier build.

  2. 02

    Choosing Framer, on the Manufact template

    Not a from-scratch build — a deliberate platform choice. Ted needed to keep editing products, prices and specs himself after handover; a hand-coded site would have locked him out of his own catalogue the day I finished.

  3. 03

    Merging the navigation into one

    One products section, one software section, one nav — instead of two sites' worth of menus that disagreed with each other about where things lived.

  4. 04

    Building the stack accordion

    The direct answer to "close to nine products, one findable page": a collapsed list that opens on demand instead of a wall of cards competing for attention.

  5. 05

    Customising until it read as CSEDS, not as a template

    Real product photography, CSEDS's own red in place of the template defaults, and copy written for an Australian engineering audience — the work that separates a site built on Manufact from a site that looks like it.

The solution

One site, built to still feel like the two it replaced

The screens below are the live site, not a prototype — full pages, scrolling for real inside the frame. That's deliberate: the whole point of this project was consolidating long content into something findable, so showing a long page scroll cleanly is the argument, not just an illustration of it.

Same content, same hierarchy, both breakpoints — proving the mobile view works isn't optional here. "Most users are on mobile" was a stated constraint from day one, not an afterthought checked at the end.

The findability answer: a stack, not a wall

Close to nine products across two former sites don't fit on one page without becoming noise. The stack accordion is the direct answer: each product collapses to a name, a photo and its headline features, and opens on demand. It's the single element doing the most work toward "findable" — the same role the team-divide screen plays in the Futee write-up.

The software arm is the clearest evidence of what "two websites into one" actually meant: a separate site — aTENA, CivilGeo Suite and DeepExcavation, three engineering-software products — folded into the same navigation and the same visual system as the materials catalogue, not left as a bolted-on subdomain.

Staying faithful

Same red, same names, same places things used to live

This wasn't a rebrand brief — it was a merge, and a merge that looks unrecognisable reads as broken, not improved. A returning customer who knew "Software" lived in the top nav, or had a product page bookmarked by name, needed to land somewhere familiar, not somewhere that looked like a different company.

Three concrete calls came out of that constraint: CSEDS's own red carried through as the accent, in place of the Manufact template's default palette — it's the brand colour Ted had already moved the business to, and swapping in a template blue would have quietly undone that. Product names stayed exactly as customers knew them — Livin Modular, Barchip, TrueGrid, TripStop — rather than being tidied up for internal consistency. And the top-level structure — Home, Software, About, Products, Contact — stayed recognisable rather than getting reorganised for its own sake, because the win here was findability within a familiar shape, not a new shape to learn.

Keeping it light

No Lighthouse score yet, but the mobile-first calls that shaped it

The site is too new to have a performance benchmark worth quoting — rather than run a number that isn't meaningful yet, or invent one, I'm naming the structural decisions that were actually made for "most users are on mobile," and leaving the measurement for later.

The stack accordion is the biggest one: collapsing close to nine products to a name and a photo each, instead of loading every product's full detail into one scroll, is a content decision that happens to also be a weight decision. Product photography is sized for a catalogue page, not treated as uncompressed hero imagery throughout. And building on Framer rather than a fully custom stack means responsive image handling and breakpoint behaviour come from a platform built for exactly this kind of content site, instead of being reinvented per page.

This is the section I'd revisit first once the site has real traffic: run Lighthouse, see what the actual number says, and treat it as the honest follow-up to a constraint that was designed for, not yet measured.

What I'd do differently

A template trust-stats block, still live, disagreeing with itself across breakpoints

Writing this case study, I went back through the live site section by section and found something I'd missed: a "Trusted by clients" block — five stock headshots, a five-star testimonial from an "Emily Carter, Product Designer," and a stat pair — sitting near the bottom of both the homepage and the software page, with a MANUFACT® watermark visible in the same section. It's unedited template content, not CSEDS's. I know because the two placements don't even agree with each other:

Neither number is real, and the fact that they contradict each other is the tell — a genuine stat wouldn't change depending on which breakpoint rendered it. It's exactly the kind of thing a discerning industrial buyer, evaluating whether to trust a supplier with a real project, would notice and hold against the whole site. It didn't make it into any of the screenshots in this write-up on purpose; it shouldn't be on the live site either.

What I'd change: a dedicated pass — after content is in, before launch — that goes hunting specifically for unedited template sections, the way you'd proofread for typos. Reviewing each new page against the brief catches missing content; it doesn't catch content that was never meant to be there in the first place.
